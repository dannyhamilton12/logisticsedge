The UK Customs Declaration Service (CDS) is the new digital platform for handling customs declarations when importing goods into or exporting goods from the UK. It replaces the longstanding Customs Handling of Import and Export Freight (CHIEF) system, bringing UK customs processes into the modern digital era.

## What is CDS?

The Customs Declaration Service is HMRC's new IT system for processing customs declarations. Unlike CHIEF, which dates back to the 1990s, CDS was built using modern technology and is designed to handle the complexities of post-Brexit trade.

CDS manages the entire lifecycle of a customs declaration, from submission through to clearance and duty payment. It serves both importers and exporters, handling:

- Import declarations (C88)
- Export declarations
- Transit declarations (NCTS)
- Post-clearance amendments
- Duty deferment and payment management

## Why CHIEF Was Replaced

CHIEF served UK trade for over 25 years, but it had significant limitations:

**Legacy Technology**: Built on 1990s architecture, CHIEF struggled to integrate with modern business systems and couldn't easily adapt to new requirements.

**Brexit Complexity**: The UK's departure from the EU required new customs processes for goods moving between Great Britain and the EU. CHIEF couldn't support these new requirements efficiently.

**Limited Functionality**: CHIEF offered basic declaration processing but lacked features like real-time status tracking, integrated risk assessment, and modern API connectivity.

**End of Life**: The EU's New Computerised Transit System (NCTS) Phase 5 transition meant CHIEF's transit functionality would no longer be compatible with EU systems.

## CDS Timeline and Implementation

The transition from CHIEF to CDS occurred in phases:

**2018-2021**: CDS launched for Northern Ireland trade and certain specialist procedures.

**2022-2023**: Full rollout for Great Britain imports, with CHIEF continuing to run in parallel.

**March 2023**: CHIEF closed for new import declarations in Great Britain.

**October 2024**: Final CHIEF closure for export declarations and Northern Ireland.

All UK customs declarations must now use CDS.

## Key Differences: CDS vs CHIEF

| Feature | CHIEF | CDS |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Data format | Proprietary | HMRC-approved data format (XML/JSON) |
| Real-time status | Limited | Full real-time tracking |
| Post-clearance amendments | Paper-based | Digital self-service |
| Duty deferment | Separate systems | Integrated |
| System interface | Terminal-based | Modern APIs and portals |

## How to Access CDS

There are three primary ways to submit declarations through CDS:

### 1. CDS Exporter/Importer Portal

HMRC's free web portal for businesses making their own declarations. Suitable for low-volume traders with simple requirements.

### 2. Commercial Software

Third-party customs software that connects to CDS via API. This is the most common solution for regular traders.

### 3. Customs Intermediary

Using a customs broker, freight forwarder, or agent to make declarations on your behalf. They access CDS through their own systems.

## CDS Declaration Requirements

CDS requires more detailed information than CHIEF did. Key data elements include:

**Mandatory Information**:
- EORI number
- Commodity codes (8-digit for exports, 10-digit for imports)
- Customs procedure codes
- Country of origin
- Value and currency
- Weights and quantities
- Additional procedure codes where applicable

**Documentation**:
- Commercial invoices
- Packing lists
- Import/export licences where required
- Certificates of origin
- Preference documents

## Common CDS Challenges

Businesses transitioning to CDS have faced several challenges:

**Data Quality**: CDS validates data more rigorously than CHIEF. Inaccurate commodity codes, valuation errors, or incomplete information cause immediate rejections.

**System Integration**: Connecting existing ERP or warehouse systems to CDS requires technical work and thorough testing.

**Training**: Staff familiar with CHIEF processes needed retraining on CDS procedures and data requirements.

**Cost**: Some businesses faced increased costs for new software, training, or switching to customs intermediaries.

## CDS Benefits

Despite transition challenges, CDS offers significant advantages:

**Real-Time Visibility**: Track declaration status in real-time through the portal or API.

**Self-Service**: Make post-clearance amendments digitally without contacting HMRC.

**Integrated Payments**: View and manage duty liabilities, payments, and deferments in one place.

**Future-Proof**: Built to accommodate future trade policy changes, including potential new free trade agreements.

**Reduced Errors**: Enhanced validation reduces common declaration errors and subsequent delays.

## Getting Started with CDS

If you're new to CDS:

1. **Register for an EORI number** if you don't have one
2. **Choose your access method** — portal, software, or intermediary
3. **Set up your data** — ensure commodity codes, supplier details, and product information are accurate
4. **Test your processes** — make test declarations before going live
5. **Train your team** — ensure staff understand CDS requirements

## Conclusion

The Customs Declaration Service represents a significant modernisation of UK customs processes. While the transition from CHIEF required effort from businesses, CDS provides a more robust, flexible platform for managing UK trade declarations.

For most businesses, working with a customs intermediary or investing in compliant software is the most practical approach to CDS compliance. The key is ensuring data accuracy and maintaining up-to-date knowledge of customs requirements.
