## Why China-UK Trade Remains Critical in 2026

China continues to be the UK's largest single-country source of imports, accounting for roughly 9% of total UK imports by value. The product categories driving this trade remain consistent: electronics and office machinery, textiles and garments, furniture and homewares, industrial components, and consumer goods. What has changed is the compliance environment. HMRC's 2026 enforcement priorities focus on accurate commodity classification, correct valuation, and proper origin declaration. For importers, the stakes are higher. Misclassification can trigger back-duty assessments and penalties. Undervaluation attracts investigation. Missing or incorrect documentation means goods held at the border, accruing demurrage charges while you sort out the paperwork.

## Pre-Import Checklist: What You Need Before Goods Leave China

Do not wait until your shipment arrives at Felixstowe or Southampton to sort out your compliance. By then, it is too late. Complete these steps before your supplier loads the container.

### Obtain Your GB EORI Number

Every business importing goods into Great Britain needs an EORI (Economic Operators Registration and Identification) number. The format is GB followed by 12 digits and a 3-digit suffix. You apply at gov.uk/eori and typically receive it within 3–5 working days. Without it, you cannot file a customs declaration. For a complete guide to EORI registration and requirements, see /articles/eori-number-uk-complete-guide/.

If you also trade with Northern Ireland, you may need an XI EORI number for movements under the Northern Ireland Protocol. For China-GB imports, the GB EORI is sufficient.

### Classify Your Goods with the Correct Commodity Code

Every product has a commodity code — a 10-digit number that determines the duty rate, any licensing requirements, and whether anti-dumping duties apply. The UK Trade Tariff at trade-tariff.service.gov.uk is the authoritative source. Do not rely on your supplier's classification; they have no liability for UK duty. For detailed guidance on classification, see /articles/uk-commodity-codes-tariff-classification-guide/.

For example, office machinery and parts often fall under Chapter 84. But the exact code depends on the product's function, composition, and technical specifications. Misclassification is one of the most common errors importers make, and HMRC's compliance teams actively target it. If you are unsure, consider binding tariff information (BTI) from HMRC — a legally binding ruling on your product's classification.

### Verify Your Supplier's Documentation Capability

Your Chinese supplier must provide specific documents, and they must be accurate. The commercial invoice is the foundation of your customs declaration. It must include:

- Full description of goods matching the commodity code
- Correct Incoterms 2020 designation (FOB Shanghai, CIF Felixstowe, etc.)
- Accurate transaction value in GBP or USD
- The exporter's Chinese Customs Registration number
- Country of origin stated as "China" or "PRC"

The packing list must show gross and net weights, number of packages, and dimensions. The bill of lading serves as title to the goods and evidence of the contract of carriage. For regulated products — electronics, machinery, toys, medical devices — you also need a Declaration of Conformity showing UKCA or CE marking compliance.

### Check for Anti-Dumping Duties and Licensing Requirements

Certain product categories from China attract anti-dumping duties on top of the standard duty rate. Steel products are the most prominent example, with duties ranging from 30% to 60% depending on the specific product and manufacturer. The UK Trade Tariff shows which codes carry these additional charges.

Some goods require import licences — controlled drugs, certain chemicals, military goods, and some agricultural products. Check the Import Licensing Database before shipping. If a licence is required and you do not have one, your goods will be refused entry.

## Calculating Landed Cost: Duty, VAT, and Hidden Charges

The price you pay your supplier is only part of the cost. You must budget for duty, VAT, freight, and port charges to understand your true landed cost.

### Duty Rates and How They Apply

The UK operates a Global Tariff Regime with three main duty rates for imports from China: standard rate (typically 4–12% depending on the commodity code), preferential rate (zero or reduced duty under trade agreements — not applicable to China post-Brexit), and anti-dumping/countervailing duty (additional duty on specific products). Duty is calculated on the customs value — generally the transaction value plus freight and insurance to the UK border (CIF value). For a complete guide to UK import duty rates and calculations, see /articles/import-duty-uk-complete-guide/.

### VAT at 20% on Aggregate Value

Import VAT is charged at 20% on the aggregate value: CIF value plus duty plus any other charges payable before release. This is not optional. You pay it at the border unless you use postponed VAT accounting. For a detailed explanation of import VAT and postponed accounting, see /articles/uk-import-vat-explained/.

Postponed VAT accounting lets you account for import VAT on your VAT return rather than paying it at the border. You still owe the VAT, but it improves cash flow. To use it, you must be VAT-registered in the UK and include your VAT number on the customs declaration. You then claim the import VAT as input tax on the same return, effectively neutralising the cash impact.

### The De Minimis Threshold in 2026

Historically, shipments under £135 were exempt from duty. In 2026, HMRC tightened enforcement of this threshold. Almost all B2B shipments from China now attract duty regardless of value. The £135 threshold still exists in legislation, but in practice, HMRC expects duty declarations on commercial imports of any value. Do not assume your small shipment is exempt — declare it.

### Freight, Port Charges, and Demurrage

Ocean freight from China to UK ports varies by route and container type. A 40ft container from Shenzhen to Felixstowe typically costs £3,000–£5,000 depending on market conditions. Port handling charges add £200–£400. Demurrage — charges for containers left at the terminal beyond the free period — can run £50–£100 per day after the first 3–5 free days.

## Documentation Requirements: What HMRC Expects

Your customs declaration is only as good as the documents supporting it. HMRC may request these documents months after clearance, so keep them for at least four years.

### Commercial Invoice

The commercial invoice is the primary document for customs valuation. It must show:

- Seller and buyer details with full addresses
- Invoice date and number
- Complete description of goods
- Quantity and unit price
- Total value and currency
- Incoterms 2020 designation
- Country of origin
- Exporter's Chinese Customs Registration number

HMRC cross-checks invoice values against known market prices. Significant undervaluation triggers investigation. If your invoice price is much lower than comparable imports, expect questions.

### Packing List

The packing list supports the invoice and helps customs officers verify the physical shipment. It must show:

- Gross and net weights
- Number and type of packages
- Dimensions of each package
- Marks and numbers on packages
- Reference to the commercial invoice number

Discrepancies between the packing list and the actual cargo can lead to physical inspection, which adds days to clearance and incurs inspection fees.

### Bill of Lading

The bill of lading is issued by the carrier and serves three functions: receipt for the goods, evidence of the contract of carriage, and document of title. For customs purposes, it proves the goods were shipped and shows the route. Ensure the consignee details match your EORI registration exactly.

## Customs Declaration Process: CDS and the TRE Service

All UK import declarations are filed through the Customs Declaration Service (CDS). From 31 March 2026, the Trader Risk Engine (TRE) service becomes the sole route for customs data submission, replacing the older MSS reporting system. For a step-by-step walkthrough of the customs clearance process, see /articles/customs-clearance-uk-step-by-step-guide/.

### Who Files Your Declaration

You can file your own declaration if you have the knowledge and CDS access. Most importers use a customs broker or freight forwarder. The broker acts as your indirect representative, filing the declaration on your behalf and assuming joint liability for its accuracy.

If you use a broker, you still need to provide them with accurate information. They rely on your invoice, packing list, and commodity code instructions. If you give them wrong information, you are liable for the resulting errors.

### Declaration Data Elements

A full import declaration contains over 70 data elements. The critical ones for China imports are:

- **1/10:** Procedure requested (e.g., release for free circulation)
- **2/1:** Simplified declaration indicator (if applicable)
- **3/1:** Declarant details (your broker or you)
- **3/2:** Importer EORI number
- **4/3:** Calculation base for taxes (CIF value)
- **4/8:** Payment method for taxes
- **6/1:** Commodity code (all 10 digits)
- **6/5:** Gross mass
- **6/8:** Country of origin
- **8/5:** Destination country
- **11/1:** Transaction type
- **14/1:** Declaration date
- **44:** Additional information, including any licences or certificates

### Timeline and Clearance

Standard clearance takes 2–6 hours if the declaration is complete and accurate. HMRC may select the shipment for inspection — either documentary (they ask for more paperwork) or physical (they examine the cargo). Documentary inspections add 1–2 days. Physical inspections add 3–5 days plus inspection fees. You can track your declaration status through CDS.

## Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Importers make the same mistakes repeatedly. Here are the most common errors and how to avoid them.

### Misclassification of Goods

Using the wrong commodity code is the single most expensive mistake. It can mean underpaying duty (leading to back-duty assessments and penalties) or overpaying (wasting money). Always verify the code against the UK Trade Tariff. If the product is complex or you are unsure, apply for BTI from HMRC.

### Missing the Exporter's Customs Registration Number

Since 2025, HMRC has required the Chinese exporter's Customs Registration number on the commercial invoice. Invoices without it are rejected, delaying clearance. Make this a mandatory field in your purchase orders and check every invoice before shipment.

### Incorrect Incoterms Application

Incoterms define who pays for what and when risk transfers. If you buy FOB Shanghai but your supplier quotes CIF Felixstowe, you are paying for freight twice — once embedded in their price and once to your forwarder. Ensure your contracts and invoices use Incoterms 2020 correctly and consistently. For a visual guide to Incoterms and risk transfer points, see /articles/incoterms-explained-visual-uk-guide/.

### Undervaluation

Some importers try to reduce duty by declaring a lower value than they actually paid. HMRC has access to pricing databases and knows what similar goods sell for. Undervaluation attracts penalties of up to 100% of the duty evaded, plus interest. It is not worth the risk.

### Ignoring Anti-Dumping Duties

Anti-dumping duties apply to specific products from specific manufacturers. If you import steel products from China without checking whether anti-dumping duties apply, you may face a surprise bill for 30–60% of the value on top of standard duty. Always check the UK Trade Tariff for your commodity code before shipping.

## Case Study: Typical Shipment from Shenzhen to Felixstowe

To illustrate the process, consider a typical shipment of office machinery parts from Shenzhen to Felixstowe.

**Product:** Printer components, commodity code 8443.99.90.90
**Value:** £25,000 FOB Shenzhen
**Freight:** £3,200 to Felixstowe
**Insurance:** £150
**Incoterms:** FOB Shenzhen

**Step 1: Calculate CIF value**
- FOB value: £25,000
- Freight: £3,200
- Insurance: £150
- CIF value: £28,350

**Step 2: Calculate duty**
- Duty rate for 8443.99.90.90: 0% (most office machinery parts enter duty-free)
- Duty payable: £0

**Step 3: Calculate VAT**
- Aggregate value: £28,350 (CIF) + £0 (duty) = £28,350
- VAT at 20%: £5,670

**Step 4: File declaration**
- Importer provides invoice, packing list, and bill of lading to broker
- Broker files declaration through CDS
- HMRC releases goods after 4 hours (no inspection selected)

**Step 5: Pay charges**
- VAT of £5,670 accounted for via postponed VAT accounting
- Broker fee: £75
- Port handling: £280

**Total landed cost:** £28,705 plus £5,670 VAT neutralised through postponed accounting. Had the commodity code been wrong, or the invoice missing the exporter's registration number, the shipment could have been held for days.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need an EORI number to import from China?**
Yes. Every business importing goods into Great Britain must have a GB EORI number. You cannot file a customs declaration without one. Apply at gov.uk/eori before your first shipment. It typically takes 3–5 working days to receive.

**What is the import duty rate for goods from China?**
The duty rate depends on the commodity code. Most consumer goods fall in the 4–12% range. Some products, like certain electronics, enter at 0%. Steel products may attract anti-dumping duties of 30–60% on top of the standard rate. Check the UK Trade Tariff at trade-tariff.service.gov.uk for your specific product.

**Is there a de minimis threshold for imports from China?**
The £135 threshold still exists in legislation, but HMRC tightened enforcement in 2026. In practice, almost all B2B shipments from China now attract duty regardless of value. Do not assume your shipment is exempt — declare it.

**How long does customs clearance take?**
Standard clearance takes 2–6 hours if the declaration is accurate and complete. If HMRC selects your shipment for inspection, add 1–5 days depending on whether it is a documentary or physical inspection. Delays also occur if documentation is missing or incorrect.

**Can I use postponed VAT accounting for imports from China?**
Yes, if you are VAT-registered in the UK. Postponed VAT accounting lets you account for import VAT on your VAT return rather than paying it at the border. You must include your VAT number on the customs declaration and report the import VAT in Box 1 of your VAT return.

**What documents do I need to keep and for how long?**
Keep all import documentation — commercial invoices, packing lists, bills of lading, customs declarations, and certificates — for at least four years. HMRC may audit your imports and request these documents. Digital copies are acceptable if they are complete and legible.
