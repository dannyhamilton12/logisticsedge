## What Is UK CBAM and Why It Matters

The UK Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) places a carbon price on certain imported goods. It ensures imported products face similar carbon costs to UK-produced goods, reducing carbon leakage — where production moves to countries with weaker climate rules.

Importers must purchase certificates reflecting the embedded greenhouse gas emissions in their goods. If goods were produced in a country with an equivalent carbon price, you can claim a reduction. This levels the playing field for UK manufacturers who already pay carbon costs under the UK Emissions Trading Scheme.

For logistics professionals, CBAM introduces new compliance obligations. You will need to collect emissions data from suppliers, calculate CBAM liability, submit quarterly declarations to HMRC, and surrender certificates. This sits alongside existing work on [customs clearance](/articles/customs-clearance-uk-step-by-step-guide/) and [import duty calculations](/articles/import-duty-uk-complete-guide/).

The Finance Act 2026 granted HMRC powers to administer and enforce CBAM. According to HMRC's February 2026 policy summary, the mechanism supports UK net zero targets while protecting domestic industry from unfair competition.

## Timeline and Implementation

UK CBAM goes live on 1 January 2027. Preparation needs to start well before then.

**Key dates:**

- **1 January 2027:** CBAM becomes operational, with rates published quarterly
- **Q1 2027:** First CBAM rates published (covering January–March imports)
- **May 2027:** First declarations due for Q1 2027 imports
- **Autumn 2026:** Illustrative CBAM rates will be published
- **2029 at earliest:** Indirect emissions may be included

Unlike the EU's CBAM, which had a reporting-only transitional phase from October 2023, the UK brings both reporting and financial obligations into force simultaneously from January 2027.

CBAM declarations follow a quarterly cycle. Importers must submit declarations within one month after each quarter ends: Q1 by 30 April, Q2 by 31 July, Q3 by 31 October, and Q4 by 31 January.

Use the period before January 2027 to establish data collection processes with suppliers. Emissions verification takes time — if you import covered goods from multiple suppliers, start requesting emissions data now.

## Which Goods Are in Scope

UK CBAM applies to five industrial sectors with high emissions intensity and significant carbon leakage risk.

**Covered sectors:**

1. **Iron and steel** — Bars, rods, angles, sections, wire, plates, sheets, coils, and tubes. Covers most semi-finished and finished steel products.

2. **Aluminium** — Unwrought aluminium, bars, rods, profiles, wire, plates, sheets, foil, and tubes. Both primary and secondary products are in scope.

3. **Cement** — Clinker and Portland cement used in construction.

4. **Hydrogen** — Compressed, liquefied, and chemically bound hydrogen.

5. **Fertiliser** — Nitrogenous fertilisers such as ammonium nitrate and urea.

Each sector is defined by specific commodity codes (HS codes). HMRC will publish a full list of in-scope codes. Cross-reference your import records against this list.

Goods that underwent sufficient processing in the UK or EU before re-export and re-import may be exempt. CBAM rules of origin differ from standard preferential origin rules used in [trade agreements](/articles/uk-trade-agreements-list/). Processing that changes the HS code may remove CBAM liability, but simple repackaging will not.

Check the commodity code against HMRC's published list. When in doubt, seek clarification from a [customs broker](/articles/customs-broker-vs-freight-forwarder/) with CBAM experience.

## How CBAM Works

CBAM operates through a certificate system administered by HMRC. Importers must purchase certificates corresponding to the embedded emissions in their imported goods.

**The CBAM process:**

1. **Import goods** in a covered sector
2. **Obtain emissions data** from the foreign producer (embedded emissions per tonne)
3. **Calculate total embedded emissions** (emissions per tonne × tonnes imported)
4. **Purchase CBAM certificates** equal to total embedded emissions
5. **Submit quarterly declaration** to HMRC with import volumes and emissions data
6. **Surrender certificates** to cover declared emissions

Each certificate represents one tonne of CO2 equivalent emissions. The certificate price is based on the UK carbon price under the UK Emissions Trading Scheme. The government will publish CBAM rates quarterly.

Embedded emissions include direct emissions from production — for example, fuel combustion in a steel furnace. Indirect emissions (from electricity used in production) are excluded until 2029 at the earliest.

If goods were produced in a country with a carbon price, you can deduct the carbon price paid abroad. You will need evidence such as tax receipts or compliance certificates. This prevents double charging.

The surrender process mirrors existing customs procedures. You will access HMRC's CBAM service to report imports and surrender certificates. Failure to surrender sufficient certificates results in penalties.

## Calculating Your CBAM Liability

CBAM liability uses a straightforward formula, but obtaining accurate emissions data from suppliers is often challenging.

**CBAM calculation formula:**

```
CBAM liability = (Embedded emissions per tonne × Tonnes imported) − Carbon price paid abroad
```

**Example calculation:**

A UK importer brings in 100 tonnes of steel bars from a country with no carbon price. The producer reports embedded emissions of 2.5 tonnes CO2 per tonne of steel.

- Embedded emissions: 2.5 tonnes CO2/tonne × 100 tonnes = 250 tonnes CO2
- Carbon price paid abroad: £0
- CBAM certificates required: 250 certificates

If the CBAM certificate price is £50 per tonne CO2, the importer pays £12,500 for this shipment.

For comparison, the EU CBAM certificate price in Q1 2026 was €75.36 per tonne CO2. UK rates may differ based on the UK carbon market price.

**Default values:** If your supplier cannot provide verified emissions data, HMRC will apply default emission values, typically set at a conservative (high) level. Using default values increases your CBAM costs, so obtain actual data from suppliers.

Perform the calculation for each shipment and aggregate for quarterly declarations. High-volume importers should consider automated systems to track CBAM liability across multiple suppliers.

## Reporting Requirements

CBAM reporting follows a quarterly cycle aligned with the calendar year.

**Quarterly declaration deadlines:**

| Quarter | Period | Declaration Due |
|---------|--------|-----------------|
| Q1 | 1 Jan – 31 Mar | 30 April |
| Q2 | 1 Apr – 30 Jun | 31 July |
| Q3 | 1 Jul – 30 Sep | 31 October |
| Q4 | 1 Oct – 31 Dec | 31 January |

Each declaration must include:

- Total quantity of each goods type imported (in tonnes)
- Total embedded emissions (in tonnes CO2e)
- Any carbon price paid abroad and supporting evidence
- Number of CBAM certificates to be surrendered

Submit declarations through HMRC's CBAM service. The system calculates certificates required based on your reported data. You must then surrender certificates from your CBAM account.

**Verification requirements:** Emissions data must be verified by an accredited verifier from 2027. The framework mirrors EU CBAM, requiring independent confirmation of reported emissions. Confirm your suppliers can provide verified data before the first deadline.

Retain all CBAM-related records for at least four years: supplier emissions declarations, verification certificates, carbon price evidence, and declarations submitted to HMRC. HMRC may audit these records.

Small importers below a de minimis threshold may face simplified reporting. The exact threshold is unconfirmed but expected to align with existing customs simplifications.

## UK CBAM vs EU CBAM

Businesses trading with both the UK and EU must understand how the two CBAM regimes interact.

**Key differences:**

| Feature | UK CBAM | EU CBAM |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Start date | 1 January 2027 | October 2023 (transitional), 2026 (financial) |
| Covered sectors | 5 sectors | 6 sectors (includes electricity) |
| Indirect emissions | Excluded until 2029 | Included from 2026 |
| Declaration frequency | Quarterly | Quarterly |
| Threshold | TBD (de minimis expected) | €150 per shipment |

For GB-EU trade, CBAM treatment depends on goods origin. EU-origin goods imported into the UK may be exempt under equivalence arrangements. UK-origin goods entering the EU may also be exempt.

However, goods originating outside both the UK and EU — for example, Chinese steel imported into the EU, then re-exported to the UK — may face CBAM charges in both jurisdictions, creating potential double charging.

The UK government will seek equivalence agreements to reduce duplication. Until then, assume both regimes apply independently. Collect emissions data satisfying both UK and EU verification requirements.

Review supply chains to identify where CBAM obligations arise. UK-manufactured goods exported to the EU are not subject to EU CBAM on return, but third-country goods may trigger obligations in both markets. Understanding [rules of origin](/articles/rules-of-origin-uk-explained/) for CBAM is essential.

## Preparing Your Business

CBAM compliance requires changes to procurement, data management, and customs processes.

**Preparation checklist:**

1. **Identify in-scope imports** — Review import records to determine which products fall within the five covered sectors. Cross-reference commodity codes against HMRC's list.

2. **Contact suppliers** — Request embedded emissions data from all in-scope suppliers. Explain CBAM requirements. Many EU suppliers already collect data for EU CBAM.

3. **Verify data quality** — Ensure supplier data meets HMRC verification standards. From 2027, emissions data must be verified by an accredited verifier.

4. **Set up internal systems** — Establish processes to track CBAM imports, calculate liability, and manage certificate purchases. This may require ERP or customs software updates.

5. **Train staff** — Ensure customs, procurement, and finance teams understand CBAM obligations. The mechanism requires cross-functional coordination.

6. **Budget for CBAM costs** — Factor certificate costs into landed cost calculations. For high-emission goods, this could add significant cost per tonne.

7. **Register for CBAM** — HMRC will open CBAM service registration before January 2027. Register early.

Businesses compliant with EU CBAM have much groundwork in place. Data collection and verification processes are similar, though UK-specific declarations are still required. If you use a [customs broker](/articles/customs-broker-vs-freight-forwarder/) for EU CBAM, ask whether they can handle UK CBAM.

## Compliance and Penalties

HMRC enforces CBAM using powers granted under the Finance Act 2026. The penalty regime mirrors existing customs enforcement.

**Penalties for non-compliance:**

- **Failure to submit declaration:** Fixed penalty plus daily penalties for continued non-compliance
- **Under-reporting emissions:** Penalty based on certificates not surrendered, plus interest
- **Providing false information:** Civil penalties up to 100% of CBAM liability, with potential criminal prosecution for deliberate fraud
- **Failure to surrender certificates:** Penalty equal to unpaid CBAM charge plus additional fines

HMRC will take a proportionate approach during initial implementation, focusing on education before enforcement. However, legal obligations apply from day one.

HMRC may review CBAM declarations during routine customs audits, checking that reported emissions match supplier documentation and certificates were surrendered correctly. Maintain complete records for at least four years.

If you discover an error in a submitted declaration, notify HMRC promptly. Voluntary disclosure may reduce penalties. The CBAM service includes mechanisms for correcting previous declarations.

For complex supply chains or high-value imports, consider seeking professional advice. The cost of advice is small compared to potential penalties and operational disruption.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When does UK CBAM start?**
UK CBAM starts on 1 January 2027. First declarations are due by May 2027 for Q1 2027 imports. The Finance Act 2026 provides the legal framework, and HMRC will publish CBAM rates quarterly.

**Which goods are subject to CBAM?**
CBAM applies to five sectors: iron and steel, aluminium, cement, hydrogen, and fertiliser. Each sector is defined by specific commodity codes. HMRC will publish a full in-scope list. Goods below a de minimis threshold may be exempt.

**How do I calculate CBAM costs?**
Multiply embedded emissions per tonne by quantity imported, then by the CBAM certificate price. Deduct any carbon price paid in the country of production. HMRC publishes CBAM rates quarterly.

**Do I need to report indirect emissions?**
Not initially. Indirect emissions (from electricity used in production) are excluded until 2029 at the earliest. Only direct emissions from the production process need reporting during the initial phase.

**What if my supplier cannot provide emissions data?**
HMRC will apply default emission values if verified supplier data is unavailable. Default values are typically higher than actual emissions, increasing your CBAM costs. Obtain actual data from suppliers and ensure proper verification.

**How does UK CBAM interact with EU CBAM?**
The two regimes operate independently. Goods may face CBAM charges in both the UK and EU if they originated outside both jurisdictions. Equivalence agreements may provide relief for UK or EU-origin goods. Collect emissions data satisfying both regimes' requirements.
