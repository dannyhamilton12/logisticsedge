## The UK Import Licensing Framework

The UK operates a two-tier import licensing system. Most goods enter freely under the Open General Import Licence (OGIL). Controlled goods require specific licences issued by the Import Licensing Branch (ILB) of the Department for Business and Trade.

The OGIL came into force on 21 October 2022, replacing the EU-style licensing regime after Brexit. It was updated in April 2025 to reflect changes in trade policy and security requirements. The OGIL permits the importation of all goods into the United Kingdom, subject to specific exceptions listed in the licence document.

Controlled goods fall outside the OGIL. You must obtain a specific import licence before bringing them into the UK. The controlled list covers goods that pose risks to:
- Public health and safety
- National security
- Environmental protection
- Animal and plant welfare
- International obligations (sanctions, non-proliferation treaties)

Importing controlled goods without a licence is a criminal offence. HMRC can seize the goods. You can face unlimited fines and up to two years' imprisonment for serious breaches.

## Open General Import Licence (OGIL): What It Covers

The OGIL is a blanket licence that covers most imports. You do not need to apply for it or reference it in your customs declarations. It applies automatically.

The OGIL covers:
- Consumer goods (clothing, electronics, furniture, toys)
- Industrial machinery and equipment
- Raw materials (metals, plastics, textiles)
- Food and beverages (except those with specific restrictions)
- Vehicles and vehicle parts
- Most chemicals (except controlled precursors and weapons-related substances)

The OGIL does not cover:
- Goods listed in the controlled goods schedule (see below)
- Goods subject to anti-dumping or countervailing duties (separate rules apply)
- Goods under trade sanctions or embargoes
- Goods requiring other government department approvals (e.g., medicines, pesticides)

You can read the full OGIL document on GOV.UK. It is updated periodically. Check for updates before importing new product lines.

## Controlled Goods Categories

The UK lists 22 categories of controlled goods requiring import licences. Each category has specific licensing requirements and competent authorities.

### 1. Excise Goods

Excise goods require licences from HMRC, not the Import Licensing Branch. This category includes:
- Alcohol (beer, wine, spirits)
- Tobacco products
- Fuel duty goods (petrol, diesel, biodiesel)

You must be registered with HMRC as an excise trader. Duty is suspended during transit and becomes payable when the goods are released to free circulation.

### 2. Controlled Drugs

Controlled drugs are listed under the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971. This category includes:
- Cocaine, heroin, morphine
- Cannabis and cannabis resin
- LSD, MDMA (ecstasy), amphetamines
- Certain prescription medications (when imported commercially)

Licences are issued by the Home Office. Import for personal use is not permitted under commercial licences. Medical and scientific imports require specific justification.

### 3. Drug Precursor Chemicals

Precursor chemicals are used to manufacture controlled drugs. They are categorised:
- **Category 1:** Highest risk (e.g., lysergic acid, P-2-P)
- **Category 2a:** High risk (e.g., acetic anhydride, ergometrine)
- **Category 2b:** Medium risk (e.g., piperidine, safrole)
- **Category 3:** Lower risk but still controlled (e.g., acetone, toluene, sulphuric acid)
- **Category 4:** Monitoring only (e.g., hydrochloric acid, sodium hydroxide)

Categories 1, 2a, and 2b require import licences. Categories 3 and 4 require registration and reporting but not licences for most imports.

### 4. Toxic Chemicals (Chemical Weapons Convention)

Schedule 1 chemicals under the Chemical Weapons Convention are prohibited except for very limited purposes:
- Sarin, soman, tabun (nerve agents)
- Mustard gas (blister agent)
- Ricin (biological toxin)

Licences are issued only for research, medical, or protective purposes. The Department for Business and Trade assesses each application.

### 5. Endangered Species (CITES)

The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) protects listed animals and plants. The UK implements CITES through the Control of Trade in Endangered Species (COTES) regulations.

CITES-listed species include:
- All great apes, elephants, rhinos, tigers
- Many parrots, reptiles, and amphibians
- Certain orchids, cacti, and cycads
- Coral, seahorses, and certain fish species

Import permits are issued by the Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA). You must prove the specimens were legally acquired and that the import will not harm wild populations.

### 6. Fishery Products

Certain fishery products require catch certificates to prove they were not caught illegally:
- Tuna, swordfish, and other high-value species
- Products from countries with IUU (illegal, unreported, unregulated) fishing warnings

The Marine Management Organisation (MMO) validates catch certificates. Without a valid certificate, the goods cannot be imported.

### 7. Anti-Personnel Mines

Anti-personnel mines are banned under the Ottawa Treaty. Import is prohibited except for:
- Mines for detection training
- Mines for forensic analysis
- Mines for destruction purposes

Licences are issued by the Export Control Joint Unit (ECJU).

### 8. Explosives and Explosive Precursors

Explosives require licences under the Explosives Regulations 2014. This category includes:
- Dynamite, TNT, plastic explosives
- Fireworks (commercial quantities)
- Ammunition and propellants
- Precursor chemicals (nitric acid, hydrogen peroxide above certain concentrations)

The Health and Safety Executive (HSE) assesses storage and transport arrangements before issuing licences.

### 9. Firearms

Firearms imports are controlled under the Firearms Act 1968. This category includes:
- Pistols, rifles, shotguns
- Air weapons above certain power limits
- Certain military-style semi-automatic firearms
- Components and ammunition

Licences are issued by the Home Office. You must hold appropriate firearms certificates in the UK before importing.

### 10. Military Goods

Goods specifically designed or modified for military use require licences. This category includes:
- Military vehicles and aircraft
- Body armour and helmets
- Night vision equipment
- Military communications equipment
- Components specifically designed for military platforms

The Export Control Joint Unit (ECJU) assesses applications against the UK's strategic export licensing criteria.

### 11. Nuclear Materials

Nuclear materials require licences under the Nuclear Safeguards Act 2000. This category includes:
- Uranium (enriched or depleted)
- Plutonium
- Medical radioisotopes
- Nuclear reactor components

Licences are issued through the digital 'apply for an import licence' service. The Office for Nuclear Regulation (ONR) assesses security and safeguards arrangements.

### 12. Offensive Weapons

Offensive weapons are defined as items designed to kill or inflict serious injury. This category includes:
- Knives with concealed blades
- Knuckledusters
- Swordsticks
- Certain martial arts weapons

Import for commercial sale is prohibited. Limited licences are available for museums, theatrical productions, and historical reenactment.

### 13. Realistic Imitation Firearms

Realistic imitation firearms (RIFs) are controlled under the Violent Crime Reduction Act 2006. Import is permitted only for:
- Film and television production
- Theatre and historical reenactment
- Airsoft skirmishing (with appropriate membership)

Importers must be registered with the Home Office as approved RIF importers.

### 14. Torture Equipment

Equipment designed for torture or capital punishment is banned under the Torture (Abolition of Death Penalty and Prohibition of Torture Equipment) Act 1989. This category includes:
- Electric chairs
- Lethal injection equipment
- Certain restraints designed for prolonged immobilisation

No licences are issued. Import is prohibited.

### 15. Ozone-Depleting Substances and Hydrofluorocarbons

Ozone-depleting substances (ODS) and certain hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) are controlled under the Montreal Protocol and UK F-Gas regulations. This category includes:
- CFCs, HCFCs (largely banned)
- Certain HFCs used in refrigeration and air conditioning

Licences are issued by the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra). Import quotas apply.

### 16. Rough Diamonds (Kimberley Process)

Rough diamonds require Kimberley Process Certification Scheme (KPCS) certificates. The scheme prevents conflict diamonds from entering the legitimate trade.

Certificates are issued by the Kimberley Process authorities in the country of export. UK importers must register with the Export Control Joint Unit.

### 17. Steel Products (Safeguards)

Certain steel products are subject to tariff-rate quotas under the UK's steel safeguards regime. Imports above the quota face additional duties.

This is not a licensing requirement but affects import costs. Check the UK Trade Tariff for your commodity code to see if safeguards apply.

### 18. Anti-Dumping and Countervailing Duties

Goods subject to anti-dumping or countervailing duties are not licensed but require additional declarations. This regime targets goods sold below fair value or subsidised by foreign governments.

Common targets include:
- Certain ceramics from China
- Biodiesel from Argentina and Indonesia
- Steel products from various countries

Check the UK Trade Tariff for your commodity code.

### 19. Sanction Goods

Goods subject to UK trade sanctions require licences. Sanctions apply to:
- Specific countries (e.g., Russia, Belarus, Iran, North Korea)
- Specific entities and individuals (listed under UK sanctions regulations)
- Specific goods (e.g., luxury goods to certain countries, dual-use goods to military end-users)

The Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI) and Export Control Joint Unit administer sanctions.

### 20. Weapons of Mass Destruction Related Goods

Dual-use goods that could contribute to weapons of mass destruction programmes require licences. This category includes:
- Certain chemicals and biological agents
- Nuclear-related equipment and technology
- Missile technology
- Certain electronics and computers

Licences are assessed against the UK's strategic export licensing criteria.

### 21. Plant and Plant Products (Phytosanitary)

Certain plants and plant products require phytosanitary certificates and import licences. This category includes:
- Seeds for planting
- Fresh fruits and vegetables from certain countries
- Wood and wood packaging material
- Certain cut flowers

The Border Trade Matching Service (replacing the Automatic Licence Verification System in February 2026) verifies phytosanitary certificates at the border.

### 22. Animal Products (Sanitary)

Animal products require health certificates and, in some cases, import licences. This category includes:
- Meat and meat products
- Dairy products
- Fish and fishery products (separate from CITES)
- Eggs and egg products

The Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA) issues import licences for high-risk products.

## How to Apply for Import Licences

### Step 1: Identify the Correct Licensing Authority

Different goods are licensed by different authorities:

| Goods Category | Licensing Authority |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Most controlled goods | Import Licensing Branch (ILB) |
| Controlled drugs | Home Office |
| Firearms | Home Office |
| Nuclear materials | Office for Nuclear Regulation (ONR) |
| Endangered species (CITES) | Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA) |
| Excise goods | HMRC |
| Ozone-depleting substances | Defra |
| Military goods | Export Control Joint Unit (ECJU) |

### Step 2: Check if You Need a Licence

Not all imports within a controlled category require a licence. Check:
- The specific commodity code
- The country of origin
- The end use
- The quantity

Some goods are licensed only above certain thresholds. Some countries are exempt from certain licensing requirements under trade agreements.

### Step 3: Prepare Your Application

Applications typically require:
- Your EORI number
- Detailed product description
- Commodity code
- Country of origin
- Quantity and value
- End use declaration
- Supporting documents (technical specifications, end-user certificates, etc.)

For nuclear materials, you must also provide:
- Security arrangements
- Safeguards commitments
- Transport plans

### Step 4: Submit Through the Digital Service

Most licences are applied for through the GOV.UK digital service. Some categories (controlled drugs, firearms) have separate application processes.

Processing times vary:
- Standard controlled goods: 20 working days
- Nuclear materials: 40 working days
- CITES permits: 15 working days
- Controlled drugs: 28 working days

### Step 5: Use the Licence in Your Declaration

Once granted, reference the licence in your customs declaration:
- Box 44 (Documents produced): Enter the licence reference number
- Include a copy of the licence with your customs documentation

The Border Trade Matching Service automatically verifies certain licences (phytosanitary, some controlled goods) against HMRC systems.

## Common Mistakes and Compliance Tips

### 1. Assuming OGIL covers everything

The OGIL is broad but not universal. Check the controlled list before importing new products.

### 2. Importing before the licence is granted

Licences are not backdated. If you import before the licence is issued, the goods can be seized.

### 3. Using expired licences

Import licences have validity periods. Check the expiry date before each shipment.

### 4. Incorrect commodity codes

The licence is tied to specific commodity codes. If your code is wrong, the licence does not cover your goods.

### 5. Ignoring end-use conditions

Some licences are granted for specific end uses only. Using the goods for a different purpose breaches the licence.

### 6. Not keeping records

You must keep copies of import licences for at least four years. HMRC can request them during audits.

### 7. Overlooking post-Brexit changes

Some licences that were automatic under EU rules now require UK applications. Check current requirements.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Importing controlled goods without a licence can result in:

- **Seizure of goods:** HMRC can seize goods at the border
- **Fines:** Unlimited fines for serious breaches
- **Imprisonment:** Up to two years for certain offences (controlled drugs, firearms, WMD-related goods)
- **Loss of trading privileges:** Repeat offenders can be barred from importing
- **Criminal record:** Convictions affect future business operations

HMRC and the Import Licensing Branch conduct compliance audits. They can request licence documentation for any import within the past four years.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need an import licence for every shipment?**
No. Most goods are covered by the Open General Import Licence (OGIL). You only need a specific licence for controlled goods.

**How long does an import licence last?**
Licences typically last 12-24 months, depending on the goods category. Some licences (e.g., for controlled drugs) are shipment-specific.

**Can I transfer an import licence to another company?**
No. Import licences are company-specific. If your business structure changes, you must apply for new licences.

**What if my goods arrive before the licence is granted?**
The goods will be held at the border. You cannot clear them through customs without a valid licence. Arrange the licence before shipping.

**Are import licences free?**
Most import licences are free. Some categories (e.g., CITES permits) have application fees.

**Can I apply for a licence after the goods have shipped?**
You can apply, but the licence will not be backdated. Goods imported before the licence date are in breach of regulations.

**Do I need a licence for samples?**
Samples of controlled goods generally require licences, though simplified procedures may apply for low-value, non-commercial samples.

**How do I know if my product is controlled?**
Check the UK Trade Tariff for your commodity code. It will indicate if a licence is required. You can also contact the Import Licensing Branch for guidance.
